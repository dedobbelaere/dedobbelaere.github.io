> Pidmohylnyi's *The City* is many things, but, at its most basic, it is a novel about Kyiv, a great and ancient Ukrainian city that \[Pidmohylnyi] lived in for barely a decade. It sits, as the novel makes clear, at the very heart of Ukrainian culture and identity. As I write these words, Russia is conducting a genocidal war against Ukraine. Kyiv is slowly being destroyed, because the Russian goal - a Ukraine that is not Ukrainian - cannot be achieved as long as Kyiv exists. We can only hope the Russians fail.
> 
> *Maxim Tarnawsky, March 2022, foreword to his translation of Valerian Pidmohylnyi's* The City

I watched the full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 begin through a computer monitor. There were videos all over the internet, posted as soon as they became available, of explosions and firefights throughout Ukraine. The invasion dominated the news for months. The torrent of information and concern over the war even changed our language. The full-scale invasion brought attention to the \#KyivNotKiev campaign, which had been live since 2018, and now Kyiv is the canonical English spelling of the Ukrainian capital.

Much of the reporting on the full-scale invasion, now that it has clawed its way into its fifth year, is published in Ukrainian or Russian. English news outlets, for the most part, only cover major strikes, though there are missiles flying into Kyiv almost every day. The little reporting that is published in English is dominated by cursory analyses of attacks and counter-attacks or ride-alongs with Ukrainian drone warfare units, marveling at the young men at computer terminals using videogame controls to defend their country.

I do not want to suggest that this reporting is unimportant. It is valuable for citizens in a modern democracy to know the broad arc of the war and its rough form in order to make informed political decisions. But I have found that when this sort of reporting dominates, it omits something, and in its omission, it can lead one away from an important truth. This sort of reporting fails to capture the familiar, human element of the war. It fails to bring the reader in touch with the people of Ukraine. 

It is easy to see the people of Ukraine, the people of Kharkiv, Kherson, and Kyiv, as mere victims. When the story of the war is told in lives lost and ground gained, the great mass of people knotted into the war can become accessories to the narrative, rather than its vital essence. It is when this happens, that sympathy can give way to pity, and pity can give way to indifference. Feeling pity can feel like a way of fulfilling one's moral duty, a way of satisfying the moral demands of the moment, abdicating one of responsibility to take action.

It is impossible to fall into this emotional and moral alienation after reading Valerian Pidmohylnyi's *The City*. The book is like many early 20th century modernist works. It is concerned with young people adapting to city life, and with societies adapting to brave new ideas. The book's medium for exploring these themes is the city of Kyiv, and Pidmohylnyi infuses the city with life, with liveliness. As I read I found myself walking through the streets of Kyiv, swimming in the Dnipro, frequenting the city's cinemas, and falling in love with a city that I have not been within a thousand miles of.

Importantly, I find myself enamored not with *any* Kyiv, but with a *Ukrainian* Kyiv. Maxim Tarnawsky's translation preserves many of the grammatical idiosyncrasies and stylistic flair that is present in the original Ukrainian. Moreover, Pidmohylnyi's Kyiv is one that overflows with Ukrainian culture and identity. Its inhabitants do not eat pelmeni, but varenyky. They revere Shevchenko, not Pushkin. They speak Ukrainian, which is not Russian, nor is it a dialect of Russian, nor is it a subsidiary of the Russian language.

When Ukraine, and especially Kyiv, are familiarized in this way, the moral horror of the war become sharper. The daily reports of those attacked and killed become more difficult to read. The refusal of the United States to send sufficient aid to Ukraine becomes a national disgrace, an act of complacency in an immoral incursion into sovereign territory by the Russians. The incursions of Russian propaganda to the Western media become more jarring, and more disconcerting.

Yet the war is still far away. The bombs do not fall on my house, the drones do not fly over my head. My town hasn't been occupied by an invading army, who replace schoolteachers with propagandists and elected officials with corrupt regime loyalists. I don't have family or friends living in Ukraine for whom I have cause to worry.

At least, not directly. My roommate is Ukrainian. He grew up in a small town near Lviv, and attended Taras Shevchenko National University of Kyiv where he received a bachelor's and master's degree in linguistics. He left Ukraine in 2022, after the invasion, and made his way to Rochester, where he is working on a Ph.D. in computational linguistics. While I do not have family and friends in Ukraine, my roommate does. Many nights I come down from my room to find him at the kitchen table, messaging his girlfriend while she shelters in the Kyiv subway, riding out another wave of Russian drone strikes.

I do not pity my roommate, but I do worry for him. Whenever I read about a drone strike on Kyiv, my first thoughts are of him. I think of his girlfriend in Kyiv, and his family, and how much he stands to lose from this war each time Russia launches another swarm of drones. In many ways my roommate is my conduit for the human stakes of the war. He is, in fact, the reason I read *The City* in the first place. I had asked him about recommendations for Ukrainian literature and he bought four books for the living room bookshelf so I could get acquainted with the Ukrainian canon.

Thus the war, despite its distance, is on my mind often. I also find myself thinking about what I ought to do in light of the war. About what anyone so far away from the war ought to do. A quote from Pynchon's *V.* comes to mind: "Love with your mouth shut, help without breaking your ass or publicizing it, keep cool, but care." As Tim Kreider sez: like a lot of wisdom, this sounds at first suspiciously similar to idiotic nonsense. Surely each of us bears responsibility for promoting peace, stopping war, and ensuring a stable geopolitical environment. How could I just sit back and let the war continue?!

This is, of course, not how geopolitics works. Some people are more responsible than others. The Kremlin, the White House, and the armies of statesmen and diplomats thereof, bear more responsibility than some graduate student with a blog for bringing this war to an end. The levers of power are out of reach, for the most part, to ordinary citizens. I am poorly situated to convince the Russian government to accept a full and unconditional surrender.

Nonetheless there are things to be done. I read about the war as much as I can. I follow domestic political developments regarding aid to Ukraine and diplomatic efforts to end the war. I try to familiarize myself with Ukrainian culture, and with the cities and towns that are under attack. I do what I can to argue against isolationism when I encounter it. I try not to let idealism paralyze me into inaction. I unfurl the Ukrainian flag that flies in front of our house whenever the winds wrap it around the flagpole.

I try to not preach in these posts, but I'll offer a few pieces of information that might be useful if this piece has moved you. First, most Ukrainians consider the start of the war to be in 2014, with the annexation of Crimea and invasion of the Donbas. 2022 marks the start of the full-scale invasion, not the start of the war. This is the fifth year of the full-scale invasion, but the thirteenth year of the war.

Second, it is important to avoid the mindset that this war is the sole responsibility of Putin, and that once he's gone we can be friends with Russia. The Russian nationalist movement is much larger than Putin, and even opposition figures like Alexei Navalny have expressed anti-Ukraine views. An excellent piece on this subject can be found [here](https://thechrissampson.substack.com/p/the-good-russian-is-not-coming-to).

Lastly, it is important to seek out the Ukrainian perspective whenever possible. The Kyiv Independent or Ukrainska Pravda, which both publish in English, are great sources. Also, Stanislav Aseyev's *In Isolation* is an excellent collection of essays from his time living in the Donbas during the initial invasion in 2014.

My intention here is not to make anyone feel guilty for not dedicating their lives to the cause of the Ukrainian people. I do intend, however, to pique your interest. I think about this war, about Ukraine, and about what I can and can't do with regards to it every day. I'd be better off not thinking about it alone. So, read *The City*. Read *In Isolation*. Talk to me about it. Talk to your friends about it. Ask a coworker if they want to try kvas and talk about foreign military aid. We cannot pull the levers of power, but we can talk about them, examine them, and observe their movement with a more careful eye. We can help without breaking our asses. We can keep cool, but care.
