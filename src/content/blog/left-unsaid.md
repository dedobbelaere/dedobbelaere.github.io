A very old friend and I talk with each other rather frequently. In the past month or so leading up to our last talk I had been feeling, in that subpersonal reservoir of emotions that haven't yet been attached to language, that there was something that I wanted to talk about with him. I talked about various anxieties, about distal and proximal goals, about whatever I could drag out of my daily quota of worries to resolve the tension. Despite my best efforts, the simmering anxiety that something was being left unsaid remained.

I am loathe to leave things unsaid. Maybe that's part of the reason why I write. Omissions and half-truths and vagaries eat at me. Especially when there are things that I can feel or understand or intuit but cannot find the vocabulary to express. Writing, as a habit, gives me the time and space to find the vocabulary I need to express something. I can ruminate on an idea until it's ready for verbal regurgitation.

I don't express everything, of course. I am not an exemplar of perfect honesty. Sometimes this is a matter of tact. Some things must be left unsaid in order to maintain the carefully curated social relationships that any person living within fifty miles of another person must maintain. Sometimes it is a matter of ignorance. Our minds are mysterious, and fickle. Important thoughts get thrown out while trivial facts get sewn into our memory. Much of our mental activity is carried out by subconscious phantoms operating behind closed doors. There are things we know deep in our gray matter, but which never make it to the surface.

Leaving these sorts of thoughts unsaid is often harmless. But not always. A few years ago I was dating a woman who I had little in common with. We rarely talked about anything substantive, but we had chemistry, and bonded over her cats. After a couple months in that relationship I began to feel restless. I had been spending more and more time with this woman, and had less and less time to myself. There are few people who would describe me as an introvert, but I nonetheless appreciate periodic solitude. I was not, at this time, aware that this was precisely what was bothering me.

What I could have done is had a frank conversation with this woman about these restless feelings and tried out some sort of different routine to see if things changed. I did not, dear reader, take this most reasonable route. I let the restlessness build week over week, until I became so desperate for something to change in my life that I broke up with this woman on February 15th. Note that Valentine's day is February 14th. I am not particularly proud of this timing. That relationship would have ended anyways, to be sure. We weren't right for each other. But, had I been able to express what had really been bothering me it could have continued somewhat stably for a few more months. At the very least I could have waited until March to end it.

Not everything left unsaid is mentally inaccessible. Sometimes something is left unsaid because it simply can't be put out in the open. Most often this, for me, is something that I find to be true but hurtful. Not cruel, or malicious, but nonetheless something that I think will wound whomever is the subject or object of them. Part of me thinks that this apprehension is some species of cowardice. I think, as a general rule, that knowledge of true things is a good. Thus, leaving things unsaid, however unpalatable those utterances might be, is to deprive someone of knowledge of some truth, and is thus in some way wrong. Leaving things unsaid might avoid an uncomfortable conversation, but giving voice to the truth trumps comfort.

Now, as I write this, I can feel resistance from the postmodern avant-garde. Assuming the things I find so difficult to say are personal matters, like my own thoughts and opinions about a person or their habits, then all that I am depriving my interlocutors of is my own subjective truth. What I should be concerned about is speaking authentically, and if I only feel that I should say something because of some external structure, then I would not be acting inauthentically by leaving that thing unsaid.

But I am firmly opposed to the idea that truth is relative or subjective. I think that there is a world outside the walls of my skull, and that it is shared by all of us. There are things that we feel or experience that can't be shared directly with others. I can't (yet) directly link my brain with other people so that they can feel what it is like to be in my head. But there are things that are true independent of our feelings and experiences. Even the fact *that* we feel or experience something is something that is true in this observer-independent way. 

If it is a fact *that* we feel or experience something, then we have reason to express that thing to the person who is the subject or object of those feelings or experiences, especially if the thing that we feel or experience bears on the substance of the relationship in question. This does not mean we have to express every thought we have about someone to that person. As I mentioned above, we must be able to maintain different sorts of social relationships to function in the world, which requires some amount of restraint. But in our most intimate relationships a disposition towards restraint can be caustic, or catastrophic.

A couple years ago I was dating a woman whose father died unexpectedly about a month into our relationship. She was devasted, naturally, and the grief from his death became a major part of our relationship. I did my best to support her, I took time off work to help her family in the weeks after her father's death, I comforted her when I could. I empathized with her grief, but I couldn't understand it. The emotions she was feeling were beyond my experience.

As the months wore on, I continued to do my best to support her, but I was limited. It is difficult to empathize with something that you don't understand. Empathy slowly became pity. She did not want to be pitied. She didn't deserve to be pitied. But that was how I felt by the end of our relationship. I lied to her, regularly, and told her that I didn't pity her. I could feel it weighing on our relationship. You can only hide so much from a person, even less when you are romantically entangled. I am certain that the pity I felt towards her in some way, however minor, shaded my behavior around her.

The relationship ended for a variety of reasons, but one of them was certainly that I was incapable of continuing a relationship with someone I pitied. There is more to say about this relationship, but I have digressed enough already. The topic merits its own blog post, one day. Oh, and if you thought I had learned my lesson about timing from the breakup discussed above then you, dear reader, would be wrong. I broke the news to her while she was in the bath.

As with the other relationship discussed in these pixels, I don't think we were meant for each other anyways. I do, however, feel that I wronged her in an important way by trying to spare her from my true feelings. I failed to express  (and, in fact, lied about) something that she likely would have found significant in the context of our relationship. I deprived her of a truth that she may have found unappealing, but nonetheless valuable.

I started this essay with a reference to a very old friend, and something important that I felt needed to be discussed but which evaded my conscious awareness. Eventually I did figure out what it was that I had wanted to discuss with him, and it was a great relief to finally get his thoughts on it. As always, he gave me sage advice. Getting that advice required me to be somewhat vulnerable, and to take the time and effort to carefully parse my thoughts and feelings. In trivial matters it is often valuable to leave something unsaid. We stand to gain little from telling our coworkers that their new haircut is atrocious. We stand to gain much more with the people we love.
