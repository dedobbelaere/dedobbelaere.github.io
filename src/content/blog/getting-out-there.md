I have been going through it for the past four days. What precisely 'it' is is unimportant for the time being. If you've been following this blog for a bit you know that I write with prolix gusto about my hard knocks, so you can trust that when the time is right a complete account will be offered here. Let's focus instead on the immediate effects.

The most significant thing I have noticed is a new desire for hermeticism. The thought of going out and talking to another person, besides a few select friends to whom I have been venting, has been mortifying. I have instead felt compelled to (*inter alia*): compress myself into such a compact space that my body mass collapses in on itself, thereby forming a black hole; move to a small mining outpost in Siberia and start a new life as the town's bookseller (low income but strong work-life balance); and stare anywhere from hundreds to thousands of miles into the distance while my psyche works things out.

I've found myself taking lots of walks, with my headphones on and baseball cap pulled low, hoping that nobody recognizes me. This would be easy if I was in Rochester, where most of my friends are off travelling during the summer break. Unfortunately, I'm also travelling, visiting my parents in suburban Chicago, in a small town where everyone knows everyone and everyone wants to chat. Circuitous, nocturnal walks are encouraged by such treacherous territory.

This is not normal for me. I am the son of two salespeople. Yapping with other intelligent creatures is in my blood. I do not often avoid small talk with old neighbors. I can hardly resist small talk with strangers. Twice in the past year I have exchanged contact information with the person sitting next to me on a flight after talking through the whole journey.

So when, as I was leaving my house for another brooding stroll, I saw my lovely neighbors of 26 years and felt the impulse to hide behind a tree, I knew that the impulse was better pushed aside. I gave them a wave and the familiar provincial social ritual began. I enquired after their affairs, they enquired after mine. I got some love from their elderly Australian cattle dog. After a few minutes my parents walked out of the house and joined in. A third neighbor stopped while driving by and greeted the party. As the small talk slowly ran out, I took my leave and started on my walk.

I wish I could tell you that the simple joy of community cured me. It did not. I still felt heavy, tense, and turbulent. I did, however, gain the small joy of dodging a regretful, albeit minor, mistake. Had I dodged my neighbors I would have drawn back ever so slightly from the world. The dispositions that comprise our character are formed by repeating these sorts of decisions over and over. In vulnerable times, it is all too easy to make the antisocial choice over and over again, so that though the storm passes the disposition to shun the broader world remains.

All things in moderation. If serious thought is to be undertaken, as is required by such troubled times, one must have a room of one's own. I'm not going to start knocking on my neighbor's doors like a Jehovah's Witness who needs to make a sale. But I'm not going to say no to crashing the afterparty of an acquaintance's wedding.

The wedding took place downtown, and the reception was at one of the local country clubs, less than a mile from my house. I didn't attempt to crash the reception, as I didn't bring my tuxedo back home to my parents' house. Lesson learned. But when a friend of mine who was at the wedding informed me the reception afterparty would be at a similarly nearby bar, what could I do but offer myself as a designated driver?

Less than one-hundred and fifty words ago I advocated for all things in moderation. But, if you've read my piece on getting sober, you know I'm not the type of person to practice moderation in a bar. I passed on the booze and got loaded on schmooze. Even after so many years sober, I haven't forgotten how to keep time with the rhythm of the night. I bounce around, I pay my respects to the bride and groom. There is no substitute for the warm rush of a tequila shot, except perhaps the joyous surprise of someone you've known since you were five recognizing you through the long hair and mustache.

I help my buddy dodge his ex-girlfriend. I enquire after other people's affairs, they enquire after mine. I realize I have an excellent idea for tomorrow's blog post. I get a number of exceedingly kind compliments on this blog (thank you, dear reader). I commiserate with those present who are also going through it. Solipsism is all too easy to adopt when one is going through it. Your it will, naturally, dominate your thoughts. Company for misery is an effective salve. Apply twice daily, whether you feel like it or not.

The night slowly wraps up. We close down the bar. Most people are taking Ubers back to the city, where most children of the suburbs present at the wedding now live. I'm driving my friend who informed me about the afterparty back to his parents' house in the suburbs. This friend is close friends with the bride and groom, who, it turns out, are also staying in the suburbs tonight. 

So it happened that I drove the bride and groom home on their wedding night. Like a pleasant conversation with some neighbors, this is not a cure when going through it. Seeing the happy couple off to their honeymoon is, nonetheless, a potent analgesic.

I'm going back to Rochester on Wednesday. I'm not looking forward to being alone with my thoughts for a nine hour drive. But I've got to get back. There's a party on Saturday. I'll see some friends I haven't seen for a while. It's going to get better with time.
